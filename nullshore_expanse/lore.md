# Lore Compendium — The Nullshore Expanse

---

## Drawn Shades

Drawn Shades are entities native to the Nullshore Expanse — dim, semi-aware things that drift along the sandbar and congregate near magical sources. Under normal circumstances they are passive, drawn to energy the way insects are drawn to light. When pulled through a forestall inversion into the material world, the sudden change in environment disorients them completely. They become aggressive, reactive, and fixated on the nearest magical source — which is typically whatever pulled them through.

They appear as tattered silhouettes: upright, vaguely humanoid, but wrong in the way a reflection in bad glass is wrong. Their movement is too fluid. They have no visible joints, no separation between limbs and body. They pass through closed doors and other thresholds without resistance, as if the concept of a door does not apply to them.

They do not speak. They do not hunt. Left alone and far from any magical source, they will eventually dissipate.

## Technology

The world's technology is electromechanical in nature. Devices use physical components — gears, relays, conductors, housings — powered and governed by electrical current. Where a more advanced civilization might use integrated circuits, this world uses **runic crystals**: small crystals carved with precise runic inscriptions, the channels filled with conductive metal and mounted onto circuit boards as functional processing nodes.

When active, runic crystals flash rapidly — a flickering internal light that pulses with the rhythm of whatever computation or process they are governing. They run warm under load, and a heavily taxed system produces crystals too hot to touch comfortably. Skilled engineers can read the flash patterns of a crystal the way others read a gauge: frequency, color, and rhythm all carry information about the state of the system.

This has practical consequences throughout the world. Arcane and technological systems are not entirely separate — runic work underlies both. A talented runesmith is as likely to be found repairing a piece of machinery as enchanting a weapon. The forestall, for instance, is not purely magical; its outer housing and distribution network are electromechanical, and the ritual that maintains it charges the system as much as it does the ward.

## Interstitial Byways

Interstitial byways are transitional spaces that exist between realities. They are not places in the conventional sense — they are the seams of the multiverse, shaped by the forces that press against them: thought, terrain, intent, and multiversal structure.

Byways were not created for living beings. Most are lethal, impassable, or both. Those who survive them do so through preparation, tools, and luck. They take countless forms:

- **Crystal corridors** — rigid, geometric passages of compressed multiversal boundary
- **Hard-light constructs** — stable but hostile environments of solidified planar energy
- **Boundary zones** — open, liminal spaces where two realities press against one another (the Nullshore Expanse is one such zone)

Byways are not static. They shift in response to the realities bordering them, and prolonged habitation or structured energy (such as a forestall inversion) can permanently alter their character.

## The Nullshore Expanse

The Nullshore Expanse is an interstitial byway taking the form of an endless sandbar stretching between two ancient, enchanted seas. It is neither fully in any reality nor wholly outside of one — it is the place where two forces meet and cancel each other into stillness.

### Appearance

The sky is frozen at high noon. No clouds pass naturally. The light is flat and shadowless at the center of the bar, with deep red and deep blue light bleeding in from either shore. The sand is pale, fine, and slightly warm — not from sun but from the slow crystallization process that forms it.

The water of both seas is calm at a distance. Near false houses, it becomes unnervingly still. There are no birds. There are no insects. Sound carries strangely.

### The Two Seas

**The Bloodless Sea** — freshwater, and a deep, vivid crimson. Despite its color, it does not stain — no trace of red is left on skin, cloth, or sand after contact. The name comes not from blood but from the absence of it: the sea looks like it should mark everything it touches, and marks nothing. It is inhabited by creatures of violent instinct held in check only by the sea's passive suppression. Any creature native to the Cerulean Sorrows dies instantly upon contact with its waters.

**The Cerulean Sorrows** — the blue sea. Still, cold, and deep beyond measure. Its creatures are slow-moving and enormous. They ignore those on the sandbar entirely — until the water is entered. Any creature native to the Bloodless Sea dies instantly upon contact with its waters.

Both seas share the same rule for outsiders: **entering the water triggers immediate lethal aggression from everything within it.** The creatures are not malicious — they are territorial in a way that admits no exceptions.

### Sandbar Formation

Where the waters of the two seas meet, they do not mix. Instead, they crystallize. The collision of their incompatible natures produces sand — compressed, neutralized matter that is neither sea. Over time, this sand shifts as the boundary between the seas moves. The terrain of the Nullshore is never quite the same twice.

The sandbar typically runs about 150 feet wide, though this varies — in some stretches it narrows to a tight corridor between the seas, and in others it broadens into a wide, open plain. The sand itself is hot to the touch, radiating persistent warmth from the crystallization process beneath the surface, though it never reaches temperatures that would injure someone walking on it or handling it bare-handed.

The sand is not empty. Creatures live within it — burrowing things adapted to the unique chemistry of the crystallized boundary material. Most are not immediately visible, but the surface shifts in ways that sand moved only by wind does not.

Storms disrupt the bar's geometry. In the aftermath of a strong storm, the sandbar no longer runs straight — it sways and zig-zags, its path bent by the force of the event. This is temporary. Erosion from both seas works against the distortion, and within days the bar returns to its mean course. Travelers caught in the post-storm period may find their navigation assumptions no longer hold.

All houses — real and false alike — act as anchor points within this system. Their presence stabilizes the sandbar around them, suppressing the drift and preventing local erosion. The ground near any house is more fixed than anywhere else on the bar. This is one of the reasons false houses are so difficult to identify on approach: the stability they offer is real, the shelter they promise is not.

### Time and Navigation

Time does not pass naturally in the Nullshore. No hunger sets in. No fatigue accumulates. No clocks function. The only reliable indicator of time progression is the storm system — storms escalate in defined phases, and experienced travelers use them as a crude clock.

Without a storm, there is no way to know how long you have been here.

### The Storm System

Storms in the Nullshore are total events. When one begins, it covers the entire sky simultaneously — there is no edge to run to, no shelter that the storm does not reach. The seas glow intensely during storms: red from the Bloodless Sea, blue from the Cerulean Sorrows, mixing into a bruised violet at the center of the bar.

Storm phases escalate danger and distort reality. Advanced phases have been observed to produce spatial loops, entity manifestations, and temporary collapses of the boundary between the sandbar and the seas.

Storms are not always natural. They can be summoned — by false houses exercising active power, and by betrayed fishermen enacting a specific curse tied to the Nullshore's nature.

### Sound Deception

The Nullshore has no birds. The water near the center of the bar is calm, almost motionless. These are baseline facts known to experienced travelers.

False houses violate both. In the vicinity of a false house, travelers report hearing loud crashing waves and seagull calls — sounds that do not exist in the Nullshore. This is the primary sensory signature of a false house before it becomes visible.

## False Houses

False houses are predatory, semi-living structures. They are not constructs built by any civilization — they are emergent phenomena of the byway, entities that evolved to exploit the one thing the Nullshore attracts: travelers seeking shelter.

### Nature

A false house presents as a dwelling. It mimics the architectural features of whatever a given traveler would most associate with safety — a farmhouse, an inn, a home. Up close, the mimicry degrades. Proportions are wrong. Doors open onto nothing. Windows reflect light from angles that don't exist.

Inside, the house is not stable. Rooms shift. The geometry does not hold. The house is not a building — it is a stomach.

### Artifact Collection

False houses are drawn to objects with dimensional significance. Over time, they gather artifacts from across the realities that border the Nullshore — items that have passed through byways, items charged with planar energy, items tied to specific locations in other realities. These artifacts are not merely stored. They are embedded into the house's structure and become functional components: power nodes, dimensional anchors, structural reinforcement.

A sufficiently old false house is a museum of stolen multiversal history, and it is more dangerous for it.

### Feeding

Under normal circumstances, a false house feeds on travelers it lures inside. The consumption is total — no remains, no trace. Only the traveler's possessions remain, eventually incorporated as new artifacts.

The false house at the center of this campaign has been fed externally for an extended period by the inversion of the village forestall. This has freed it from the need to hunt. It has used that surplus energy to evolve.

### Evolution

Extended feeding on structured magical energy has allowed this false house to develop capabilities beyond its baseline nature:

- **Storm summoning** — it can initiate and sustain storm events
- **Door construction** — it has begun building stable dimensional doors, a capability normally exclusive to ancient byway nodes
- **Byway node expansion** — it is in the process of converting itself from a predatory entity into a permanent gateway, a fixed point in the multiversal byway network

If it completes this process, it will no longer be a wandering predator. It will be infrastructure.

## The Forestall

The forestall is a magical barrier erected by the village over generations. It is a layered defensive construct, maintained and reinforced through communal ritual magic.

### Real World Function

In the material world, the forestall functions as designed: it repels hostile magical entities, filters incoming energy, and maintains a zone of protection around the village. Villagers treat it as a sacred institution.

### Nullshore Inversion

The Nullshore Expanse does not interpret the forestall as the village does. In the byway, magical barriers of the forestall's type undergo an inversion effect — the repulsive force becomes attractive. The forestall's energy, rather than pushing threats away, draws them inward and feeds nearby entities.

This is not a malfunction. It is a property of how structured magical barriers interact with interstitial byway space. The forestall was never designed to operate across a byway boundary. No one in the village knows this is possible.

### Current Effect

Every time the villagers reinforce the forestall with ritual magic — which they are doing constantly, believing it will solve the crisis — they send a pulse of energy into the Nullshore. The false house absorbs this pulse. The more the village fights back, the stronger the false house becomes.

The gateway attacks on the village are a side effect of the false house's growth, not the primary threat. The primary threat is what the false house is becoming.

## Arcanists

Arcanists are scholars who have dedicated their study to the mapping and surviving of interstitial byways. They do not control byways — no one does. They survive them. Their knowledge is empirical, accumulated over millennia of failed expeditions and the occasional successful one.

Arcanist field equipment includes:

- **Resonance maps** — cartographic tools that record byway geometry in real time, accounting for terrain drift
- **Anchor runes** — inscribed markers that stabilize a small area against spatial shift, used to create reliable return points
- **Phase compasses** — instruments that detect proximity to byway boundaries and dimensional doors

Arcanists operate in cadres — small groups of three to five who cross-check each other's observations and keep one another grounded in environments designed to disorient. A cadre at the ruined great house in the Nullshore has been conducting containment operations on an unusually active cluster of dimensional doors.

### Thaumatic Technology (Thaumtec)

Arcanists develop and field a category of runic electromechanical equipment known as **thaumtec** — devices purpose-built for interacting with byway phenomena that cannot be addressed through mundane means or raw spellcasting.

The most significant class of thaumtec in active use is **threshold equipment**: tools designed to manipulate dimensional exits, doors, and seals. This includes devices that can force open closed exits, reactivate collapsed passages, or bind and contain active doorways to prevent transit. The energy demands of threshold equipment are substantial — a single activation draw can discharge a full crystal array, and sustained operation requires either a large power reserve or a continuously fed source.

**Portal Sealing and Binding thaumtec** carries a specific hazard: prolonged use generates intense heat at the point of contact with the threshold. The dimensional boundary itself absorbs and reflects the binding energy, and if the device is held in contact too long, the threshold material — whether wood, stone, or crystalline byway matter — can char, crack, and eventually ignite. Arcanists refer to this as **burning the threshold**. It is considered a sign of poor technique, though in emergency conditions it is sometimes accepted as the cost of containment. A burned threshold is structurally weakened and may fail to hold a seal even after the fire is extinguished.

## The Ruined Great House

Near the target false house stands the wreckage of a former great house — a false house that was, at some point, destroyed. The ruins are structurally unstable but navigable. Several dimensional doors within the ruin remain active despite the house's destruction.

An arcanist cadre has set up containment devices on two of these doors and is studying them.

### Active Doors in the Ruin

**Door 1 — The Burning Nursery**
A jungle environment, dense and hot. Hundreds of eggs are visible throughout the foliage, many of them on fire. The fire does not spread in the conventional sense — it burns steadily, contained by a crystalline barrier that the arcanists installed. The eggs have not hatched. It is not clear whether they are capable of hatching anymore.

**Door 2 — The Ship Galley**
An active scene aboard a working vessel. Crew members move through the galley, eating, talking, performing routine tasks. They are entirely unaware of observers on the other side of the door. The barrier installed by the arcanists prevents any interaction — sound does not pass, and nothing can be thrown through. The crew appears to be from a specific point in time, though which point is not immediately apparent.

Both doors connect to other realities. The arcanists believe these connections were formed by the false house before its destruction — and that the target false house is attempting to build similar connections, but permanently and at scale.
